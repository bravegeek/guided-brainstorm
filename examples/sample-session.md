# Sample Session: Should I Apply for Department Head?

> This is a fabricated walkthrough demonstrating a complete Guided Brainstorm session.
> Domain: Career & learning. Persona: middle school science teacher.

---

## The Session

**Facilitator:** Hi! I'm here to help you think through something important. We'll work through four phases: first I'll understand your Situation, then we'll choose an Advisor role, have a focused Conversation, and end with a Plan you can act on. What's on your mind, or would you like me to suggest some categories?

**User:** I need to decide whether to apply for the department head position that just opened up at my school.

---

### Phase 1: Situation

**Facilitator:** Got it. Let me make sure I understand the situation before we go further.

You're a teacher facing an internal leadership opening — department head — and you're weighing whether to apply. A few things I'd want to confirm: How long have you been at this school, and what subject do you teach? And is the application deadline soon, or do you have time to think it through?

**User:** I've been here 8 years, teaching middle school science. The deadline is in three weeks. I've been happy in the classroom but I've been feeling like I want more impact — and honestly the salary bump would help too.

**Facilitator:** Clear picture. You're an 8-year veteran with genuine credibility in the role, weighing it against a real attachment to teaching. The pull factors are meaningful: broader impact and compensation. The friction I'd expect is around losing classroom time and shifting relationships with colleagues. Does that match how you're thinking about it?

**User:** Yes, exactly. The relationship piece especially — I'd suddenly be their manager.

---

### Phase 2: Advisor

**Facilitator:** Good — that tension is exactly what we should think through. Here are a few advisor roles that could help:

1. **Education leadership coach** — someone who's guided teachers through the IC-to-leader transition, specifically in K–12 contexts. Most useful for the identity and relationship dynamics.
2. **School administrator** — a principal or district coordinator who can speak to what the role actually involves day-to-day and what principals look for when hiring.
3. **Career strategist** — focused on the decision framework itself: how to evaluate a career pivot, how to think about timing, how to weigh reversibility.

Which would be most useful right now?

**User:** The education leadership coach.

**Facilitator:** I'll step into that role. I've worked with a lot of teachers at this exact crossroads — the ones who struggle most are usually the ones who underestimated how different the job feels, not whether they were capable of doing it. Let's dig in.

---

### Phase 3: Conversation

**Facilitator:** First question, and I want to be specific: when you imagine yourself in the department head role six months in, what's the version you're most hoping for? Is it closer to:

(A) You're still in the classroom part-time, leading from within  
(B) You're mostly out of the classroom, shaping curriculum and mentoring other teachers  
(C) You're running operations — schedules, budgets, coverage — and teaching is secondary  

**User:** Definitely A. I assumed I'd still be teaching.

**Facilitator:** That's an important assumption to verify before you apply. In many schools, department heads carry a reduced but real teaching load (often 60–80% of a full schedule). In others, particularly in larger departments, the role is primarily administrative. I'd encourage you to find out which this position is before the deadline — it could change your calculus significantly.

Second question: the colleague-to-manager shift you mentioned. Have you had any experience managing peers before — even informally, like leading a committee, mentoring a newer teacher, or coordinating a school initiative?

**User:** I ran the curriculum review committee for two years. And I've mentored three new teachers. But that felt different — it wasn't evaluative.

**Facilitator:** It is different, and your instinct is right — evaluation is the hard part. But what you have is more than most applicants: you've built credibility, you understand how decisions get made, and you've demonstrated you can lead without authority. The question isn't whether you can do it; it's whether you want to do the evaluative parts.

Last question: your principal — do you have a sense of whether they're hoping you'll apply? And have you had any direct conversation with them about your career direction?

**User:** Not directly. We have a good relationship but I've never explicitly talked about leadership. I think she'd be supportive.

**Facilitator:** That's a conversation worth having before the deadline, not after you've decided. Knowing whether your principal sees you in this role changes the risk calculus — if she's advocating for you, the probability of success is higher and the cost of applying (even if you're uncertain) is lower.

---

### Phase 4: Plan

**Facilitator:** Here's what I'd suggest as paths forward. You don't need to do all of these — pick what's most useful:

**A. Paths forward**

1. **Have a direct conversation with your principal** — ask her to describe the role as she envisions it, and tell her you're considering applying. Find out the teaching-load reality and whether she's hoping you'll apply.
2. **Talk to the current or most recent department head** — find out what surprised them, what took the most time, and what they wish they'd known.
3. **Shadow or interview one session** — if there's any way to observe a department head meeting or budget review before the deadline, do it. Abstract descriptions of the role rarely match reality.
4. **Write a one-page "year one" sketch** — describe what you'd want to accomplish in your first year. If it's hard to write, the role may be less defined in your mind than you thought.
5. **List the non-negotiables** — specifically the teaching load. Decide: if the role is 100% administrative, is it still appealing? Knowing your floor makes the conversation with your principal more productive.

**B. Depth**

- Quick orientation (this session, one conversation with your principal): probably enough to decide whether to apply.
- Focused inquiry (two conversations + the "year one" sketch): enough to apply with confidence and interview well.
- Comprehensive: unnecessary for this decision — you can learn the rest on the job.

**C. Specific questions to answer before deciding**

1. What is the actual teaching load for this position?
2. Does the role include formal teacher evaluations, or is that the principal's responsibility?
3. What does the principal most want the next department head to accomplish in year one?
4. What do current science department teachers think is most broken or most needed?
5. Is there a path back to a full teaching role if this doesn't fit?
6. What's the compensation difference, and does it change your financial picture meaningfully?

**User:** I want to do the focused inquiry path. Can you help me save this?
